# HOMETOWN RAFTERS — RHODE ISLAND REDS
## The Choice Tree: The Gus Almeida Eras (1918–1959)
### WORKING DOCUMENT

---

## HOW THE TREE WORKS

Every era has one **Top-Tier Choice** — a massive, franchise-defining decision that reshapes the trajectory for decades. These are rare, heavy, and permanent.

Underneath that are **Second-Tier Choices** — the everyday management decisions that accumulate over the course of an era. Individually they're small. But they lean the franchise in a direction, and by the time the Top-Tier Choice arrives, the player has already been walking toward it. The big moment should feel earned or inevitable, never random.

The three paths aren't moral categories. They're orientations:

- **The Roots Path** — the franchise exists for the community that built it. Every decision prioritizes the people, the neighborhood, the identity. Harder road. Smaller budget. Deeper loyalty.
- **The Ambition Path** — the franchise exists to win and grow. Pragmatic. Not corrupt, but willing to make deals, cut corners, trade tradition for advantage. The middle road that feels reasonable until you look back and realize how far you've drifted.
- **The Money Path** — the franchise exists to survive by any means necessary, and survival means taking what's offered. The easiest road in the short term. The most catastrophic long-term.

No path is locked in permanently. The player can drift between them. But the accumulation of second-tier choices creates momentum — it gets harder to reverse course the deeper you go. A player who's been making Ambition choices for a decade will find the Money path feels like a natural next step, not a betrayal. A player who's been making Roots choices will feel physically uncomfortable when the Money option appears. That's the design goal.

**Gus is the compass.** His reactions tell the player where they stand. The Portuguese escalation system is the emotional readout of the franchise's soul.

## ERA 1: THE MILL ERA (1918–1930)
### "Wool and Dirt"
### Gus Almeida: Founding Player, Age 18–30

### THE WORLD

Pawtucket is a mill town running on textile money that's already starting to crack. The Blackstone Valley was the most heavily industrialized area in the entire United States, but by 1923 the cotton industry is falling off as production shifts south. The workers don't know it yet, but the foundation they're standing on has a timer.

The Reds are founded by a loose coalition of mill owners and workers. The stadium is a wooden bandbox in the shadow of the Slater Mill district. The roster is guys who work the mills and trades during the day and play ball on the seventh day. Gus is a plumber. Some guys are missing fingers from the loom. Nobody is getting paid enough to call this a career.

The fanbase is working-class immigrant families: Portuguese, Italian, French Canadian. The games are one of the only things that bring those communities together instead of dividing them.

### GUS IN THIS ERA

Gus is young but already himself. He plays shortstop like he runs pipe — precise, methodical, no wasted motion. Career average around .305–.310 in the dead ball era. Leads the league in stolen bases early. Fielding percentage is absurd for guys playing on gravel.

He lives a stone's throw from the Bandbox. He walks to the field. After the game he walks home. He's not flashy. He's never the MVP. The press box joke is already forming: "Gus Almeida did nothing spectacular today. He just did everything right."

He calls every player "kid." He shows love by showing up.

### SECOND-TIER CHOICES (Accumulating Throughout the Era)

These are the everyday decisions that lean the franchise toward one of the three paths:

**Community Investments**
- A Portuguese family can't afford tickets. Do you let them in free, set a precedent, build loyalty? Or do you protect the gate revenue you desperately need?
- The Italian community wants a chouriço vendor at the park. The mill owner who co-founded the team wants to keep concessions "professional." Whose side are you on?
- A French Canadian kid shows up with real talent but his family needs him in the mill. Do you find a way to get his family some money so he can play, or do you let the talent walk?

**Financial Pressures**
- The stadium needs repairs. Do you pass the hat through the community, take a loan from the mill owners (with strings), or let it deteriorate?
- A local businessman offers to sponsor the team. His money is clean but he wants his name on things and his opinions heard. How much influence do you trade for stability?
- Attendance is thin some weeks. Do you cut player "pay" (such as it is), reduce the schedule, or find creative ways to fill seats?

**Identity Decisions**
- The live rooster incident happens. Do you lean into it as the team's identity or try to project something more "respectable"?
- A sportswriter from Providence writes a condescending piece about the "little mill team in Pawtucket." Do you ignore it or make it bulletin board material?
- The team is terrible but beloved. A trade is available that would make you better but would send away a fan favorite who's been there since the founding. Gus has an opinion on this.

**How Gus Reads:**
- Roots choices: Gus plays harder. Shows up earlier. The thing just works. He doesn't say anything because he doesn't need to.
- Ambition choices: Gus is quiet. Not angry. Watchful. "Oh meu deus" under his breath at the occasional decision, but he's still engaged.
- Money choices: Not really available yet in this era — the money hasn't arrived. But if the player is maximizing profit at the community's expense, Gus starts giving you less. Fewer words. Shorter conversations. The warmth pulls back.

### TOP-TIER CHOICE: THE FOUNDATION

**What is this franchise for?**

This isn't presented as a single dramatic moment. It crystallizes from the accumulation of second-tier choices. By the end of the Mill Era, the franchise has an identity — not because the player chose it in a menu, but because a hundred small decisions pointed the same direction.

**Path A — Roots:** The franchise is a community institution. You invested in the neighborhood. You let families in when they couldn't pay. You sided with the vendors over the owners. The budget is thin, fan loyalty is deep, and the team is genuinely OF this place. Gus is thriving. The Gus Standard is the franchise's operating philosophy whether anyone's named it that yet or not.

**Path B — Ambition:** The franchise is a competitive enterprise. You made practical deals. You took the sponsorship money. You made the roster moves that made the team better even when they were unpopular. The budget is modest but stable, the fanbase is solid but transactional, and the team is good enough to matter. Gus respects the wins but something about the direction makes him uneasy. You hear "oh meu deus" once or twice a season.

**Path C — Profit:** The franchise is a business. You maximized revenue. You charged what the market would bear. You treated players as assets. The budget is the healthiest of the three paths, the fanbase is thinner but paying more, and the team has the resources to grow. Gus is still here but he's pulled back. He does his job. The warmth is cooling. He hasn't said "ah cabeça de mula" yet, but it's coming.

**What carries forward:** Baseline fan loyalty. Community relationships. The franchise's reputation in the league. Gus's trust level. The financial foundation that determines how vulnerable you are when the Depression hits.

## ERA 2: THE DECLINE AND THE CREEP (1930–1945)
### "Shadow Money"
### Gus Almeida: Player-to-Coach Transition, Age 30–45

### THE WORLD

The Depression crushes the textile industry. Mills close. Pawtucket loses thousands of residents. The team is hemorrhaging money. Attendance collapses. There are rumors every year that the Reds are folding or relocating.

In 1932, the Providence mob merges with the Boston operation — the real-world Morelli-Buccola consolidation. In the game, this mirrors the "Almost-Relocation" — Boston interests attempt to absorb the Reds. A group of local fans and "businessmen" pool resources to keep the team in Rhode Island. The saviors are heroes at the time.

The early mob infrastructure starts offering help. It's not Patriarca yet — he doesn't consolidate until the 1950s. This is the precursor. The guys with cash when nobody else has cash.

WWII takes half the roster. Replacement players. Skeleton crew. The fans who show up during the war years become the most hardcore lifers the franchise will ever know.

### THE BANDBOX COLLAPSE

Early 1930s. The Depression is crushing the franchise. The stadium is falling apart — no money for maintenance. A load-bearing section of the grandstand gives way during a game. People are in danger.

Gus doesn't think. He acts. He gets people out of the way but doesn't get himself clear in time. Breaks both his legs.

That's what ends his playing career. Not age. Not declining skills. He was still good enough to play. The decision that saved people is the decision that took baseball away from him as a player.

The story becomes front-page news across New England. Donations pour in. Attendance spikes — first out of sympathy, then genuine love. The franchise that was about to fold survives the Depression because Gus Almeida broke both his legs saving people at a crumbling ballpark.

Gus never talks about it. People bring it up, he changes the subject. He comes back the next season in the dugout. Walking with a limp he'll carry the rest of his life. Managing. Doing his job. Like nothing happened.

**This is NOT a choice.** This happens on every path. This is who Gus is. The Bandbox Collapse is a fixed narrative event that resets the emotional stakes — regardless of what path the player has been on, Gus's sacrifice reminds them what this franchise is supposed to be about. The guilt or gratitude the player feels depends on how they've been running things.

### SECOND-TIER CHOICES (Accumulating Throughout the Era)

**Survival Decisions**
- The stadium is crumbling. A "businessman" offers to fund repairs. His money comes from somewhere you don't ask about. Take the repairs or let the building rot?
- Attendance is so low the team can't pay its debts. A connected figure offers to buy a "silent" stake. He just wants to help. He's from the neighborhood. What's the harm?
- A player gets into trouble off the field. A local fixer offers to make it go away. No cost. Just a favor owed.

**The "I Know a Guy" Economy**
- You need permits fast for stadium repairs. Someone knows someone at City Hall. It's not corruption — it's how things work in Rhode Island. But now you owe a favor.
- A vendor wants the concession contract. He's connected. His food is fine but not great. The other guy has better food but no connections. Who gets the spot?
- A kid with marginal talent shows up for a tryout. His uncle is one of the "businessmen" who helped save the franchise. The kid isn't good enough. But his uncle's money is keeping the lights on.

**Wartime Choices**
- Half your roster is gone to war. Do you field replacement players and keep the stadium open (at a loss), or suspend operations and risk losing the fanbase entirely?
- A war veteran returns with diminished skills but enormous goodwill. Do you give him a roster spot he hasn't earned, or tell a hero he's not good enough?
- The war-era fans are the most loyal you'll ever have. Do you reward that loyalty when times get better, or do you treat it as a baseline and optimize from there?

**How Gus Reads:**

After the Bandbox Collapse, Gus's moral authority is absolute. He didn't just play for this team — he bled for it. His reactions carry more weight now.

- Roots choices: Gus is managing with everything he has. The limp doesn't slow him down. He's building a scouting pipeline out of nothing — neighborhood kids, guys he sees playing pickup. The farm system is held together with tape and Gus's eye for talent. "Oh meu deus" when a prospect does something raw but promising.
- Ambition choices: Gus manages. He does his job. But there's a distance. He sees the deals being made. He doesn't say anything yet. The silence is building.
- Money choices: Gus is watching from the dugout as men he doesn't trust start appearing in the owner's box. "Ah cabeça de mula" when you make a decision that trades principle for convenience. He's not gone yet. But the Portuguese is coming out more often.

### TOP-TIER CHOICE: THE MONEY

**The mob money arrives. Do you take it?**

This is the most consequential decision in the franchise's history. But it doesn't arrive as a dramatic confrontation. It arrives as a series of escalating offers that have been building through the second-tier choices for fifteen years.

If you've been on the Roots path, the money arrives and feels like a test. You've been scraping by on community goodwill and Gus's scouting eye. The franchise is alive but barely. The money would make everything easier. Refusing it means another decade of grinding. The choice is genuinely hard because the community you've been serving would benefit from the investment.

If you've been on the Ambition path, the money arrives and feels like the next logical step. You've already been making practical deals. You already owe favors. The line between "I know a guy" and "I'm in business with a guy" has been blurring for years. Taking the money doesn't feel like crossing a line because you can't see the line anymore.

If you've been on the Profit path, the money arrives and feels inevitable. You've been optimizing for survival. The connected figures have been in your orbit for years. Some of them already own pieces of the franchise through the "silent" stake. This isn't a choice. It's an acknowledgment of what's already happened.

**Path A — Refuse the Money (Available from any path, but hardest from Profit)**

You say no. The next fifteen years are brutal. The franchise scrapes by on nothing. But the foundation is clean. Gus stays. Gus manages through the war years with a roster held together with spit and stubbornness. The fan loyalty from this era is the deepest in franchise history — the people who stayed through the refusal years are lifers forever.

Gus's reaction: Nothing. He just keeps managing. But the thing works a little better. The dugout is a little tighter. He shows up a little earlier. This is the plumber's approval. You maintained the pipes right.

**Path B — Take the Money, Set Limits (Available from any path)**

You take the investment but try to control it. You set terms. You keep the connected guys out of roster decisions. You use the money for the stadium, the farm system, the infrastructure. It works — for now. The franchise stabilizes. But the terms start slipping. A "favor" here. A nephew on the roster there. The limits erode because the money doesn't respect limits.

Gus's reaction: "Oh meu deus." He sees it. He knows what it is. He doesn't leave — not yet — but the trust is fractured. He manages harder, as if he can hold the franchise's soul together through sheer effort in the dugout. The Portuguese comes out in private moments. He calls you "cabeça de mula" once, to your face, and it's the most personal thing he's ever said to you.

**Path C — Take the Money, No Limits (Available from Ambition and Profit paths only)**

You take everything offered. The stadium gets rebuilt. The roster gets talent. The farm system gets real investment. The franchise goes from dying to thriving in five years. The connected guys are in the owner's box, in the front office, in the parking lot. Everyone knows. Nobody says it out loud.

Gus's reaction: "Ah que porra." Said once. Quietly. In the dugout after a game where a connected player he can't bench costs them a win. He doesn't quit — not yet. But something in him has closed. He does his job. The warmth is gone. He's managing a franchise he doesn't recognize anymore.

**What carries forward:** The financial trajectory for the next thirty years. The moral foundation (or rot) that compounds through the Golden Age. Gus's trust level, which directly affects his exit in Era 3. The roster composition — dirty money buys talent faster but creates dependency. Clean books mean slower growth but sustainable independence.

## ERA 3: THE TRANSITION (1946–1959)
### "Sunday Gravy"
### Gus Almeida: Aging Manager, Age 46–~60ish

### THE WORLD

Post-war boom. The mills aren't coming back but there's energy in the state. Providence's jewelry industry is booming — costume jewelry capital of the world, 80% of all US jewelry production. Federal Hill is fully established as Providence's Little Italy. Raymond Patriarca takes power in 1952, moves operations to the Coin-O-Matic on Atwells Avenue in 1954.

A new owner takes over the Reds. Who this owner is depends on the path:

- **Refused the Money:** The owner is a local businessman — maybe from the jewelry industry, maybe a tradesman who made good. He's clean. He's ambitious. He wants to modernize the franchise legitimately. He respects Gus.
- **Took the Money with Limits:** The owner is the "guy who knows the don." Charismatic, beloved, connected. The stadium gets renovated with money that materialized from nowhere. He holds court on Atwells Avenue. He respects Gus but sees him as a relic of the old way.
- **Took the Money, No Limits:** The owner IS the operation's investment. He's a front. The real decisions are made elsewhere. Gus is still in the dugout but the power structure has moved completely around him. He's managing a team whose roster is partially dictated by people who don't care about baseball.

### SECOND-TIER CHOICES (Accumulating Throughout the Era)

**Roster Influence**
- A "favor" lands you a star player. He's genuinely talented. But now there's a guy on the roster who can't be cut because he's somebody's nephew. The Nephew becomes a folk figure — not hated, just accepted as the cost of doing business. His at-bats are met with resigned silence.
- A young prospect from the farm system (the Gus pipeline) is ready. But giving him the roster spot means benching someone with connections. Gus wants the kid. The owner wants peace. Who do you listen to?
- A free agent from a bigger market is available. He's better than anyone on your roster. But he costs money that only comes from one source, and signing him means deeper entanglement.

**The Federal Hill Influence**
- Sunday gravy makes its way into the ballpark from Federal Hill. Someone starts selling meatball sandwiches. The vendor is "connected" the same way everything on Atwells Avenue is connected. The food is incredible. The prices are fair because the vendor answers to people you don't ask about. Do you care?
- A sportswriter starts asking questions about the ownership structure. Do you cooperate, stonewall, or have someone "talk to him"?
- The Patriarca parallel figure wants a luxury box. He's never asked for anything directly before. This is the first explicit acknowledgment of who's really involved. Do you build it?

**Gus's Standing**
- Gus is getting older. His methods are old-school. A younger coach with modern ideas is available. Do you let Gus keep managing, move him to an advisory role, or push him out?
- Gus disagrees with a roster decision publicly. Not loudly — that's not Gus. But the beat writers notice he's not happy. Do you back him or override him?
- Gus has been developing players his entire career. His scouting pipeline produces blue-collar, fundamentally sound players who reflect his values. The new money wants flashier talent. Whose vision of the roster wins?

**How Gus Reads:**

This is the era where the player's relationship with Gus reaches its emotional climax. Every interaction carries the weight of thirty-five years of accumulated history.

- **If you refused the money:** Gus is aging but content. The franchise is smaller than it could have been. The roster is homegrown. The Gus Standard is the actual operating philosophy. He's tough on the young players. He's tough on you. But when he calls a kid "kid," there's warmth in it. He's proud of what this franchise is, even if he'd never say it.

- **If you took the money with limits:** Gus is managing through gritted teeth. He sees the nephew on the roster. He sees the money in the luxury box. He does his job because that's who he is. But the "oh meu deus" is constant now. He's fighting a rearguard action — protecting his players from the influence, keeping the dugout clean even as the front office compromises. He's tired. Not physically. Spiritually.

- **If you took the money, no limits:** Gus is a ghost in his own dugout. He manages because he doesn't know what else to do. The roster is half talent and half obligations. He can't develop players the way he wants because the pipeline gets overridden by connected signings. The Portuguese comes out daily. "Ah cabeça de mula. Ah que porra." He's talking to himself as much as anyone. The players respect him out of history, not authority. He knows it.

### TOP-TIER CHOICE: GUS'S EXIT

**How does Gus Almeida leave the franchise?**

This is not a choice the player makes directly. It's the consequence of everything they've done for thirty-five years. The path determines what happens.

**Path A — The Voluntary Farewell (Refused the Money path)**

Gus decides it's time. He's in his late fifties or early sixties. The limp is worse. He's been managing for twenty-five years. He picks his successor — someone from his pipeline, someone who learned the Gus Standard firsthand. There's no ceremony because Gus wouldn't allow one. He just stops showing up at the dugout one day and starts showing up on his porch.

He watches games from his house. Close enough to hear the crowd. He's at peace. The franchise retires his number in a small ceremony years later. Gus shows up, says almost nothing, tips his cap, goes home.

The Gus Standard is the franchise's DNA going forward. Not because anyone imposed it, but because it's what the organization grew up inside.

**Gus's last words to the player:** He doesn't say anything. He just nods. And you know that's everything.

**Path B — The Forced Retirement (Took the Money with Limits path)**

The new ownership "promotes" Gus to a ceremonial role. It's framed as an honor. Everyone knows what it is. Gus is being removed from the dugout because his stubbornness is inconvenient. He won't play the nephew. He won't change the lineup when he gets the call from the front office. He won't bend.

Gus takes it. He doesn't fight. He doesn't complain publicly. He goes home to Pawtucket. Same house. Stone's throw from the stadium. But he doesn't watch from the porch anymore. He goes inside and listens on the radio.

The franchise retires his number during the Golden Age in a ceremony that is half tribute and half guilt. Gus shows up, says almost nothing, tips his cap, goes home. The silence in the stadium after he leaves is deafening.

The Gus Standard persists as an invisible metric — everyone in the organization knows it exists, but it's becoming an aspiration rather than a practice.

**Gus's last words to the player:** "Oh meu deus." Quiet. Sad. Not angry. He expected this. That's worse.

**Path C — The Erasure (Took the Money, No Limits path)**

Gus is fired. Not ceremonially. Not gently. The connected ownership doesn't need him anymore and his resistance to their roster decisions has become a problem. A younger, more compliant manager is brought in. Gus is told his services are no longer required.

He goes home. Same house. But now the crowd noise from the Bandbox sounds different to him. The team that plays there isn't his team anymore. He stops listening to games. His neighbors see him on the porch less. The kids on the block who grew up imitating his walk still do it, but they're starting to forget why he walked that way.

The franchise doesn't retire his number during the Golden Age. Or if they do, it's a PR move by the connected ownership to buy legitimacy — invoking Gus's name to sell a vision of respectability they don't practice. Whether this feels genuine or exploitative depends on whether the player has any Gus Standard left in their soul.

**Gus's last words to the player:** "Ah que porra." Then he turns and walks away. The limp. The door. Gone.

## WHAT CARRIES INTO THE GOLDEN AGE (1960–1975)

### The Gus Standard Meter

After Gus's departure, his presence becomes a permanent background mechanic. The "Gus Standard" is a hidden stat that affects:

- **Fan loyalty depth** — fans remember what Gus stood for. A franchise that honored him has deeper roots. A franchise that betrayed him has fans who show up but don't trust.
- **Player recruitment** — clean franchise attracts players who want to be part of something real. Corrupt franchise attracts mercenaries and guys who want the lifestyle.
- **Community resilience** — when the Fall comes (and it always comes), the franchise's ability to survive depends on how much goodwill is left. The Gus Standard is the savings account.
- **NPC behavior** — the old-timer in the parking lot, the vendors, the broadcasters. They all reference Gus differently depending on the path. On the clean path, he's a saint. On the corrupt path, he's a cautionary tale. On the middle path, he's the argument that never ends.

### Which Golden Age Legend Appears

- **Refused the Money → The Heir.** Homegrown. Developed through the Gus pipeline. Chose Rhode Island over bigger markets because the franchise still means something. His legacy is uncomplicated.
- **Took the Money with Limits → The Generational Talent.** Could appear on any path, but on this path he's the guy who transcends the politics. He doesn't care about the money or the morality. He just wants to win. His legacy depends on what happens around him.
- **Took the Money, No Limits → The Golden Boy.** Electric talent. Massive personality. Lifestyle that no ballplayer's salary explains. He IS the dynasty. When the Fall comes, his legacy gets complicated forever.

### The NPC in the Parking Lot

He exists on every path. But who he is and what he represents changes:

- **Clean path:** He's Gus's spiritual heir. He sat in the Bandbox as a kid. He has his old seat in his garage. He brings it to the Providence parking lot when the team moves. He's not angry. He's faithful to what the franchise was and what it still could be. He's the conscience.
- **Middle path:** He's angry. He remembers the good years and he remembers the compromises. He's in the parking lot because the franchise broke a promise he can't name but feels in his bones. He's the unresolved argument.
- **Corrupt path:** He's bitter. He watched the franchise sell everything Gus built. He's in the parking lot because going inside would mean accepting what it became. He's the accusation. He's mad at YOU. And you carry that across every era because you're the one who made the choices that led here.
