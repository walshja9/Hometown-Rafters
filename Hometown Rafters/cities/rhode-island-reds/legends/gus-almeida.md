## GUS ALMEIDA — THE FOUNDATION

**Available on all paths. Fixed character. The franchise's moral compass.**

### The Basics

**Full Name:** Augusto "Gus" Almeida
**Position:** Shortstop (player), Manager (post-Bandbox Collapse)
**Born:** ~1900, Pawtucket, Rhode Island
**Ethnic Background:** First-generation Portuguese-American (Azorean)
**Day Job:** Plumber, 40+ years
**Playing/Managing Career with the Reds:** 1918–~1959 (founding member through voluntary farewell, forced retirement, or erasure depending on path)

### Where He Comes From

Pawtucket. Born there. Dies there. Same house for over fifty years, a stone's throw from the Bandbox. He's a plumber by trade — works during the day, walks to the field, plays shortstop, walks home. The idea of living somewhere else has never occurred to him. Not because he's limited — because he's complete.

First-generation Portuguese-American. His family came from the Azores. He exists in the immigrant community that built the Blackstone Valley — Portuguese, Italian, French Canadian families working the mills and trades, sharing food across ethnic lines at the ballpark.

### How He Plays

Shortstop. Plays the position like he runs pipe — precise, methodical, no wasted motion. Career average around .305–.310 in the dead ball era. Leads the league in stolen bases early. Fielding percentage is absurd for guys playing on gravel.

He's not flashy. He's never the MVP. The press box joke: "Gus Almeida did nothing spectacular today. He just did everything right."

### Who He Is

Tough, disciplined love. Drops everything for family. Shows love by showing up — the affection is in the action, never the words. He calls every player "kid."

A plumber's ethic applied to everything: you do the job right because doing it wrong means someone's house floods. There's no shortcut that doesn't cost someone eventually. A place for everything and everything in its place.

Stubborn in a way that's rooted, not irrational. Same house, same neighborhood, same standards.

### The Portuguese Escalation

Gus speaks English professionally. Few words, measured. The Portuguese is the real man underneath, surfacing only when emotion exceeds discipline:

- **"Oh meu deus"** — exasperation. Still engaged. The player can fix this. Everyday frustration — a bad play, a dumb decision. Gus is frustrated but still in the room.
- **"Ah cabeça de mula"** — you're being a mule head. Personal. YOU are the problem. The player hears this when they keep making the same mistake, leaning into the same bad pattern.
- **"Ah que porra"** — disgust. The line has been crossed. Moral, not strategic. Something has been damaged between Gus and the player that won't repair easily.

The escalation ladder: "oh meu deus" → "ah cabeça de mula" → "ah que porra." The player learns to read it like a temperature gauge on the franchise's soul.

### The Bandbox Collapse

Early 1930s. The Depression is crushing the franchise. The stadium is falling apart. A load-bearing section of the grandstand gives way during a game. People are in danger.

Gus doesn't think. He acts. He gets people out of the way but doesn't get himself clear in time. Breaks both his legs. It was that or watch people get crushed.

That ends his playing career. Not age. Not declining skills. The decision that saved people is the decision that took baseball away from him as a player.

He never talks about it. He comes back the next season in the dugout with a limp he'll carry the rest of his life. Managing. Doing his job. Like nothing happened.

**This is NOT a choice. This happens on every path. This is who Gus is.**

### The Gus Response System

Three tiers of reaction that the player learns to read:

**Tier 1 — The Work:** When Gus approves, he just does his job harder. Shows up earlier. Stays later. The roster plays tighter. The plumber's approval — the thing just works.

**Tier 2 — The Silence:** When the player starts drifting, Gus stops calling them "kid." Doesn't argue. Doesn't confront. Just pulls back. The warmth goes cold. Players in the dugout notice before the player does.

**Tier 3 — The Portuguese:** When the player has genuinely betrayed what the franchise is supposed to be, the English stops being enough. The Portuguese breaks through. The door is closing.

### Gus's Exit (Path-Dependent)

**Voluntary Farewell (Clean Path):** Gus decides it's time. Picks his successor from the pipeline. No ceremony. He just stops showing up at the dugout and starts showing up on his porch. Watches games from his house. He's at peace. Last words to the player: nothing. He just nods. That's everything.

**Forced Retirement (Middle Path):** The new ownership "promotes" Gus to a ceremonial role. Everyone knows what it is. Gus takes it without complaint. Goes home. Stops watching from the porch — goes inside and listens on the radio. Last words: "Oh meu deus." Quiet. Sad. He expected this. That's worse.

**Erasure (Corrupt Path):** Gus is fired. Goes home. Stops listening to games. The kids on the block still imitate his walk but are starting to forget why. Last words: "Ah que porra." Then he turns and walks away. The limp. The door. Gone.

### The Gus Standard

After his departure, Gus's presence becomes a permanent background mechanic. The "Gus Standard" affects fan loyalty, player recruitment, community resilience, and NPC behavior across every subsequent era. It degrades through corrupt choices and recovers through principled ones, but can never reach zero (Gus broke his legs for this franchise — that act exists outside the player's choices) and can never fully recover after corruption (some things, once broken, are repaired but never unbroken).

### Based On

Gus Almeida is based on the game creator's grandfather. A Portuguese-American plumber from Pawtucket who worked 40+ years until he broke his legs jumping from scaffolding — it was that or get crushed. Lived in the same house for over fifty years. Stubborn in his old age. Dropped everything for family. "Ah que porra," "oh meu deus," and "ah cabeça de mula" were his favorites when he disagreed with you.

**His Legacy in One Sentence:** A plumber who played shortstop, broke both his legs saving people, and became the standard against which a hundred years of decisions are measured.
