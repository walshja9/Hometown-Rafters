## THE BOSTON TRAITOR: BRENDAN "B-MAC" McALLISTER

### The Basics

**Full Name:** Brendan Thomas McAllister
**Nickname:** B-Mac (given by the press, adopted by the fans, eventually used against him)
**Position:** Left fielder
**Born:** ~1950 (clean/middle path, Golden Age) or ~1955 (corrupt path, late Golden Age)
**From:** Cranston, Rhode Island. Park Avenue neighborhood. Irish-Italian family — dad was a firefighter, mom worked the jewelry district. Grew up riding his bike to the Bandbox. Had a poster of Marcus Hall on his bedroom wall.

### Who He Is

Brendan McAllister is the most naturally talented position player the Rhode Island Reds have ever produced. Not Marcus Hall — Marcus was power personified, a specific instrument. Brendan is a five-tool player. He hits for average, hits for power, runs, fields, and throws. He's the kind of prospect that makes scouts use the word "complete." He's also from Cranston, which means every kid in Rhode Island knows his name by the time he's sixteen.

He comes up through the system — either the Gus Pipeline (clean path) or the connected talent acquisition network (corrupt path). On both paths, he's genuinely talented. The connections didn't create him. But on the corrupt path, the connected ownership takes credit for finding him, which taints the narrative later.

He's smart. Not intellectual — street smart. He reads rooms the way he reads pitchers. He knows when to talk and when to shut up. He's funny in the clubhouse — the kind of guy who keeps the room loose without being a clown. He married his girlfriend from Cranston West. His kids go to school in Warwick. His parents still come to every home game and sit in the same seats.

He is, for five to seven years, the most beloved athlete in Rhode Island. Not just the Reds — the state. He's the local kid who made it without leaving. He does charity work in Providence. He coaches Little League in Cranston during the offseason. He knows every usher at the Bandbox by name. He is everything the franchise wants to be: homegrown, loyal, and excellent.

### The Betrayal

Boston comes calling. A team with three times the budget, a national stage, and a contract that would set his family up for three generations. The offer arrives during the Golden Age — either the peak (clean/middle) or the decline (corrupt).

**On the high Gus Standard:** He stays. The franchise means something to him. Cranston means something to him. He takes less money — significantly less — to remain a Red. The fanbase's love becomes religious. His jersey retirement is a holy event. He finishes his career in Rhode Island and transitions into the organization — coaching, mentoring, eventually maybe a front office role. His loyalty validates every clean choice the player made. He's the proof that building something real attracts people who want real things.

**On the medium Gus Standard:** He agonizes. He loves Rhode Island but he can see the cracks. The franchise has problems — not criminal, but structural. The limits-path compromises are visible to someone inside the building. The player gets a Moment: a meeting with Brendan where the front office makes the case for staying. What the player can credibly promise — based on the franchise's actual financial state and competitive trajectory — determines Brendan's choice. If the player has enough credibility (medium-high Gus Standard), Brendan stays conditionally. If the franchise's trajectory looks uncertain, he leaves with visible regret. He cries at the press conference. Rhode Island cries with him. Then Rhode Island gets angry.

**On the low Gus Standard:** He leaves without agonizing. He looks at the franchise — the corruption, the hollow culture, the connected guys in the luxury box — and makes a rational decision. Boston is better. Everything about it is better. He doesn't hate Rhode Island. He's just done pretending it's something it isn't. His departure press conference is professional and brief. He thanks "the fans and the community" in a tone that is warm but final. The fans hear it and know — this isn't a negotiating tactic. He's gone.

### The Wound

When B-Mac leaves, Rhode Island takes it personally. Not because he's wrong — because he's right. His departure confirms the state's deepest insecurity: we're not enough. We're the place people leave. The talent goes to Boston. The money goes to Boston. The ambition goes to Boston. Rhode Island is where you're from, not where you stay.

"He pulled a B-Mac" enters the local vocabulary. It means leaving for something better when the people who believed in you needed you to stay. It's used for everything — a friend who moves to New York, a restaurant that closes, a high school coach who takes a college job. The phrase carries thirty years of accumulated abandonment anxiety compressed into four syllables.

The fans burn jerseys. Walt Callahan goes silent during the first game Brendan plays against the Reds in a Boston uniform. Dead silent. Doesn't say his name. Doesn't describe the at-bat. Just lets the boos fill the broadcast for nine innings. When someone asks Walt about it later, he says: "I don't call games for other teams' players."

### The Return

Brendan is thirty-seven. Declining. On a farewell tour. The schedule brings Boston to Rhode Island.

He walks into the visiting clubhouse at the Bandbox (or the Waterfront Park) and it feels like coming home to a house where someone died. Everything is familiar. Nothing is the same.

The franchise's response is a second-tier choice:

**Welcome him home (High Gus Standard response):**
A video tribute on the scoreboard. His career highlights in a Reds uniform. The crowd stands — not all of them, and not right away, but enough. The boos mix with applause and the applause wins by the third-base line. Brendan tips his cap. His eyes are wet. His parents are in their old seats. The old-timer, if he's alive, turns his radio up in the parking lot.

Walt or Sully's call: Dead silence for the first pitch. Then, when the at-bat ends: "Welcome home." Two words carrying everything.

**Let the fans decide (Medium response):**
No tribute. No acknowledgment from the franchise. The crowd boos for nine innings. Loudly. Personally. Every at-bat. Brendan takes it. He doesn't argue. He doesn't gesture. He plays the game and takes the bus back to the hotel and calls his wife in Cranston and says "yeah, it was bad."

The Bitter Ex-Player's call: "Yeah, he's back. He left because the franchise gave him every reason to. I can't blame him. I can't forgive him either. That's Rhode Island for you."

**Exploit it (Low Gus Standard response):**
"Revenge Game Night." Dollar hot dogs. Special pricing. The franchise turns the wound into a promotion. The crowd boos but the boos feel manufactured — more performative than genuine, because the franchise has taught them to treat everything as content.

The Corporate Hire's call: "And welcome back to a former Red! Let's take a look at his career stats, brought to you by Dunkin'." No grief. No silence. Just data and sponsors.

### The Long Echo

**If he stayed (clean path):** Brendan is a coach or advisor in the Reckoning era. He's in his sixties. He's the bridge between the Golden Age and the present. He can tell Sam Medeiros stories about Marcus Hall. He can tell Maria Sousa-Chen what the franchise felt like when it was clean. His presence in the organization IS the Gus Standard made flesh — a man who chose to stay when he didn't have to.

**If he left and was welcomed back (medium path):** Brendan retired in Boston. He lives in Cranston. He comes to Reds games occasionally — sits in the stands, not a luxury box. The relationship is complicated. The fans who were old enough to remember his departure still carry it. The younger fans know the story but don't feel the wound. His daughter grew up in Boston. She might be at the Culminating Moment — wearing Boston gear, watching from the visitor's section, a living reminder of the road not taken.

**If he left and was exploited (low path):** Brendan never comes back to Rhode Island. Not to games, not to events, not to the city. He saw what the franchise did with his departure — turned his grief into dollar hot dog night — and he's done. His daughter has never been to a Reds game. She's a Red Sox fan. On the lowest path, the franchise lost not just Brendan but his entire family's connection to Rhode Island baseball. That's a wound that doesn't heal because the franchise cauterized it with a promotion.

### His Legacy in One Sentence
The most talented player Rhode Island ever produced, who either proved that loyalty is possible or proved that it has a price — depending entirely on what the franchise built around him.

## THE FROZEN LEMONADE FAMILY: THE ARRUDA FAMILY

### The Name

**Arruda.** Portuguese surname. Common in Rhode Island — specifically in the East Bay and East Providence. Not flashy, not famous, not connected. The kind of name you see on a mailbox in Riverside or a business card in Warren. They've been in Rhode Island since the 1920s. The first Arruda came from São Miguel in the Azores and worked at a textile mill in Central Falls before his wife started selling lemonade from a pushcart.

### The Lineage

**Generation 1: Maria Rosa Arruda (~1920s–1950s)**
The matriarch. She started selling frozen lemonade from a wooden pushcart outside the Bandbox in the late 1920s because her husband's mill wages weren't enough and she could make lemonade cheaper than anyone in Pawtucket. She squeezed the lemons herself. The recipe was Azorean — less sugar than American lemonade, more tartness, a hint of something nobody could identify (it was lemon zest boiled into the simple syrup, a technique from her mother's kitchen in Ponta Delgada).

She didn't have a formal agreement with the franchise. She just showed up. Elias Ashworth tried to have her removed once — "unauthorized vendor on team property." The community response was immediate and hostile. Ashworth backed down. Maria Rosa stayed.

She sold frozen lemonade at the Bandbox for thirty years. She knew Gus. Not well — they weren't friends. But she poured him lemonade after games and he said "obrigado" and she said "de nada" and that was the extent of their relationship. A Portuguese plumber and a Portuguese lemonade vendor, existing in the same space, speaking the same language, serving the same community.

**Generation 2: António "Tony" Arruda (~1950s–1985)**
Maria Rosa's son. He modernized the cart — wheels that actually worked, a proper ice chest, a hand-painted sign. He formalized the relationship with the franchise under Sal Tessitore's ownership (Sal, being Sal, arranged it as a favor — "the lady with the lemonade, she's good people, give her a spot"). Tony expanded the menu: frozen lemonade, frozen watermelon, and during cold games, hot coffee and chocolate.

Tony is the one who served during the Longest Game. At 2 AM, when the stadium had nineteen people and the temperature was below freezing, Tony Arruda was standing at his cart pouring free coffee into paper cups. He didn't charge. Nobody asked him to stay. He stayed because the people in the building needed something warm and his cart was the only concession still operating. The frozen lemonade was locked in the ice chest. The coffee was boiling on a camp stove he brought from his garage.

He poured coffee for the nineteen fans, for the umpires between innings, for Walt Callahan's engineer who came down from the press box at 3 AM shivering. He poured coffee for Danny Couture after the suspension at 4:07 AM — Danny walked past the cart on his way to the parking lot and Tony handed him a cup without a word. Danny drank it. They nodded at each other. Another silent Rhode Island conversation.

**Generation 3: Ana Arruda-Santos (~1985–2010s)**
Tony's daughter. She took over the cart when Tony's arthritis made it impossible to grip the ladle. She married a guy named Santos from East Providence (Portuguese marrying Portuguese, the Rhode Island tradition). She updated the cart again — better equipment, a small freezer, branded cups. But the recipe didn't change. Maria Rosa's recipe. The lemon zest. The tartness.

Ana is the one who gets the form letter from Brad Whitfield's management team. "The board has approved a new beverage partnership. Your vendor contract will not be renewed." After eighty years. After the Longest Game. After three generations. A form letter.

What happens next is the player's choice — and one of the most emotionally loaded second-tier decisions in the game.

**Generation 4: Marco Arruda-Santos (~2010s–present)**
Ana's son. If the family survives Brad Whitfield's purge (because the player protected them), Marco is running the cart during the Reckoning era. He's in his twenties or thirties. He makes frozen lemonade from his great-grandmother's recipe. He's also on Instagram. He posts videos of the lemonade-making process. He has a following. The frozen lemonade cart has become a minor internet phenomenon — "the family that's been making lemonade at a baseball stadium for a hundred years."

If the grassroots ownership restores the family (Clean Slate era), Marco's return to the stadium is a diegetic event. The cart appears at its old spot. The hand-painted sign. The recipe. Ana might be there for opening day, standing behind Marco, watching him pour the first cup in the spot where her father poured free coffee at 3 AM.

If the family was removed and never restored, Marco runs a frozen lemonade stand somewhere in East Providence. Not at the stadium. The connection is severed. He makes the same recipe. The customers are different. The Arruda family's eighty-year relationship with the Reds franchise is a story his grandmother tells, not a living tradition.

### The Arruda Cart as Diegetic Indicator

| Gus Standard | The Cart |
|---|---|
| High | Prominent location. Hand-painted sign. Maria Rosa's recipe. Ana or Marco behind the counter. Fans line up specifically for Arruda lemonade. Walt/Sully mentions it on the broadcast: "Grab an Arruda on yah way to yah seat." |
| Medium | Present but relocated to a less visible spot. The recipe is the same. Fewer fans know the history. The broadcast doesn't mention it. |
| Low (pre-removal) | Squeezed into a corner. The corporate beverage sponsor has the prime location. The Arrudas are technically still there but invisible. |
| Low (post-removal) | Gone. Replaced by a corporate beverage kiosk. Nobody at the stadium remembers why the lemonade used to taste different. The kiosk serves mass-produced frozen lemonade from a machine. It's fine. It's not the same. |

The first cup poured in the restored Bandbox or at the Waterfront Park is a small, private ceremony — Marco, Ana, maybe the old-timer's granddaughter buying the first one. No press. No announcement. Just a family coming home. |

### Why "Arruda"

The name matters because it's authentically Portuguese-Rhode Island without being famous or connected. The Arrudas aren't the Almeidas (that's Gus — the foundation). They're not the Tessitores (that's Sal — the connected world). They're the third layer — the community members who aren't legends or power brokers but who show up every day and make the place work. Their name on a lemonade cart is the franchise's most humble, most persistent, most easily destroyed piece of culture. It's the thing that a Brad Whitfield would delete without thinking and a Maria Sousa-Chen would restore without hesitating. The difference between those two responses IS the Gus Standard, measured in frozen lemonade.

### Their Legacy in One Sentence
Four generations of a Portuguese family selling lemonade at a baseball stadium, whose presence means the franchise remembers where it came from, and whose absence means it forgot.
