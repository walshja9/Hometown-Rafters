# HOMETOWN RAFTERS — RHODE ISLAND REDS
## The Ownership Lineage: Seven Eras, Seven Power Structures
### CHARACTER PROFILES — WORKING DOCUMENT

---

## HOW OWNERSHIP WORKS

The player is the franchise — the institutional spirit that persists across a century. The owners are the power structure ABOVE the franchise. They set the budget. They define the constraints. They force the Top-Tier choices. They are not villains by default — some genuinely love the team. But they all have agendas that may or may not align with the franchise's soul, and the tension between what the owner wants and what the institution needs is where the drama lives.

The player doesn't choose the owners. Owners arrive as consequences of previous eras. The mill founders are a given. The "businessmen" appear because the Depression demands them. The connected charmer appears because the money created the conditions for him. Each owner is the logical product of what came before — and each one reshapes what comes after.

Owners interact with the player through the institutional framing — board memos, budget allocations, front office directives. They don't sit across a desk from the player. They issue mandates that the front office receives. The player's choices are about how to respond to those mandates within the constraints the owner creates.

## OWNER 1: THE MILL COALITION (1918–~1932)

### Not One Man — A Committee

The Reds aren't founded by an individual. They're founded by a coalition of mill owners, factory managers, and local tradesmen who see a baseball team as a civic utility — something to keep the workers happy on the seventh day, to give the neighborhood an identity, to keep the immigrant communities from tearing each other apart.

### The Members

**The Senior Partner: Elias Ashworth**
English-descended mill owner. Third generation. His grandfather built one of the first cotton mills on the Blackstone. Elias inherited it and inherited the paternalism that comes with it — he genuinely believes the workers are his responsibility, the way a father believes his children are his responsibility. He doesn't respect the workers as equals. He respects them as dependents. The baseball team is part of that worldview: give them a game on Sunday and they'll work harder on Monday.

Ashworth puts up the majority of the founding capital. He expects the team to break even, not profit. He doesn't attend games — he sends his foreman to report back. He views baseball the way he views the mill's Christmas party: a cost of doing business that keeps the machinery running.

**The Junior Partners:**
A rotating cast of smaller contributors — a Portuguese bakery owner who kicks in fifty dollars, an Italian tailor who donates fabric for the first uniforms, a French-Canadian foreman who organizes the work schedules so his guys can get to games. They have no power individually but collectively they represent the community's investment. The team belongs to them in spirit if not on paper.

### Mechanical Impact

- **Budget:** Tiny. The Coalition funds the team at subsistence level. There's no surplus. Every dollar spent on the field is a dollar not spent on the mill.
- **Constraints:** The Coalition views expenses skeptically. Stadium improvements require convincing Ashworth, who sees the Bandbox as adequate the way he sees the mill housing as adequate — functional, not comfortable.
- **The Gus Dynamic:** Ashworth doesn't know Gus personally. Gus is a name on a roster sheet. But the Junior Partners — the bakery owner, the tailor, the foreman — they know Gus. They drink with him. They watch him play. When Gus starts becoming the soul of the franchise, it's the Junior Partners who feel it, not Ashworth. The tension between Ashworth's paternalism and the community's genuine ownership of the team is the Mill Era's fundamental dynamic.
- **Exit:** Ashworth's mill closes in the late 1920s as the textile industry collapses. He sells his stake to cover debts. The Junior Partners can't afford to maintain the team alone. The franchise is suddenly without its primary benefactor, entering the Depression with no financial anchor.

## OWNER 2: THE "BUSINESSMEN" SAVIORS (~1932–~1952)

### The Almost-Relocation and the Rescue

When Ashworth's money disappears and the Depression hits, Boston interests make a play to absorb the Reds. The Almost-Relocation. A group of "local businessmen" — Rhode Islanders with cash when nobody else has cash — pool resources to keep the team in Pawtucket. They're heroes. The press calls them saviors. The community celebrates.

Where the money comes from is a question nobody asks in 1932 because asking would mean knowing, and knowing would mean choosing, and choosing would mean the team dies.

### The Figure: Salvatore "Sal the Saint" Tessitore

**Full Name:** Salvatore Antonio Tessitore
**Nickname:** Sal the Saint (given by the press after he "saved" the franchise — the irony compounds over decades)

Sal is not a mob boss. He's not even a made man. He's the layer between — the legitimate businessman who has relationships with people who have relationships with the Morelli crew and, later, the ascending Patriarca organization. He owns a restaurant supply company that provides equipment to every Italian restaurant on Federal Hill. His books are clean. His relationships are not.

Sal genuinely loves baseball. He grew up going to games at the Bandbox. His father — an immigrant from Calabria — took him as a kid and they sat in the bleachers eating chouriço because the Italian vendors hadn't arrived yet. When the franchise nearly dies, Sal steps in because the thought of Pawtucket without baseball is intolerable to him.

He's warm. He's generous. He feeds everyone. His restaurant supply trucks deliver to the Bandbox for free — "what, I'm going to charge the team?" He knows every player by name. He knows their wives' names. He knows their children's names. He is, for twenty years, the most beloved figure in the franchise's history outside the dugout.

He's also the door through which the money enters.

### Mechanical Impact

- **Budget:** Modest but stable. Sal's involvement brings financial predictability for the first time. The franchise isn't rich but it isn't dying. The money doesn't come with a visible price tag — not yet.
- **Constraints:** Sal doesn't make roster demands. He doesn't interfere with the dugout. He watches games from a seat behind home plate, not a luxury box. His influence is social, not operational — he introduces the franchise to people, and those people start doing favors, and the favors start accumulating.
- **The "I Know a Guy" Economy:** Sal is the origin point. Every connected favor in the franchise's future traces back to a relationship Sal facilitated. He doesn't see this as corruption. He sees it as community. "I know a guy who can help" is Sal's native language.
- **The Gus Dynamic:** Sal respects Gus enormously. He sees Gus as the soul of the team and himself as the body — Gus makes it beautiful, Sal keeps it alive. On the clean path, this relationship is genuine and never tested. On the corrupt path, Sal's "help" gradually involves people that Gus recognizes as dangerous, and the rupture between them — never spoken, because Sal would never confront Gus and Gus would never confront Sal — is the slow heartbreak of Era 2.
- **Exit:** Sal ages out in the early 1950s. He doesn't sell — he "transitions" the franchise to the next owner, who is either a legitimate buyer (clean path) or a connected figure who Sal vouches for because "he's a good man, he loves the team" (corrupt path). Sal dies in the late 1960s. His funeral is standing room only. Half the attendees are from the neighborhood. Half are from somewhere else.

## OWNER 3: THE CONNECTED CHARMER (~1952–~1978)

### Path-Dependent Character

This owner is fundamentally different depending on the path. On the clean path, he exists but his power is limited. On the corrupt path, he IS the franchise.

### The Figure: Nicholas "Nicky Gem" Gemma

**Full Name:** Nicholas Paul Gemma
**Nickname:** Nicky Gem (from his family's jewelry manufacturing business in the District — but also because he's polished, he's bright, he's the thing everyone wants to look at)

Nicky Gemma grew up in the jewelry district. His father built a costume jewelry operation from nothing — Coro, Trifari, Monet were the big names, but the Gemma family ran a mid-size shop that employed eighty people and produced charm bracelets that sold in every department store in America. Nicky inherited the business and the charm.

He bought the Reds because he loves being important. Not in a cynical way — Nicky genuinely delights in being the center of attention. He holds court at a restaurant on Atwells Avenue. He buys rounds for strangers. He remembers every name, every birthday, every favor owed. He is charismatic in the way that Rhode Island produces charismatic men: not flashy, but magnetic. You want to be in the room with Nicky Gemma.

### On the Clean Path

Nicky is a legitimate businessman who buys the franchise from Sal's estate. His jewelry money is real. His enthusiasm is real. His desire to win is real. But he's in over his head — the franchise needs more capital than the jewelry business can provide, and Nicky's charm doesn't translate into financial discipline. He runs the franchise like he runs his factory: personally, generously, and slightly over budget every year.

The clean-path franchise under Nicky is fun but fragile. The parties are great. The stadium improvements are ambitious. The payroll is higher than the revenue supports. Nicky is the owner who makes you love the franchise and worry about its future simultaneously.

He exits when the jewelry industry starts declining in the late 1970s. His business can't subsidize the franchise anymore. He sells to a local group — reluctantly, emotionally, at a press conference where he cries.

### On the Corrupt Path

Nicky is the front. His jewelry business is real, but it's been compromised — laundering through the plating operations, the same chemical-and-cash intersection that employed half of Federal Hill's economy. He bought the Reds with money that Sal Tessitore arranged, and the people behind Sal are now behind Nicky.

On this path, Nicky IS the Golden Age. He's the owner who builds the dynasty with money that materialized from nowhere. The stadium gets luxury boxes. The roster gets star players. The scouting pipeline gets funded. Everything works because the money never stops. And Nicky's charm — his genuine, irresistible charm — makes everyone want to believe it's legitimate.

He holds court on Atwells Avenue. The Patriarca parallel figure sits at his table. Reporters see them together and don't write about it because Nicky buys them drinks and remembers their children's names. The players love Nicky because Nicky loves them — he's at every game, in the clubhouse afterward, buying dinner for the team. Vinnie Calandrelli's lifestyle doesn't look strange in Nicky's world because Nicky's whole world looks like that.

### Mechanical Impact

**Clean Path:**
- Budget: Moderate but overextended. Revenue doesn't quite cover Nicky's ambitions.
- Constraints: Nicky wants to win and will approve spending that the books don't support. The player gets more budget flexibility but with long-term debt risk.
- No corruption mechanics. The challenge is managing enthusiasm without fiscal recklessness.

**Corrupt Path:**
- Budget: Generous. The money flows. But the Favor Economy activates fully under Nicky's ownership.
- Constraints: The connected figures start making demands. The Nephew arrives. The luxury box gets built. Roster interference begins.
- The Investigation Clock starts ticking. Nicky is insulated — he's too charming, too public, too beloved for the feds to touch directly. But the people behind him are not insulated, and the investigation circles closer every year.

### The Gus Dynamic

Nicky respects Gus the way everyone respects Gus — from a distance, with reverence. On the clean path, Nicky tries to honor Gus's legacy genuinely but clumsily, naming things after him without understanding what the name means. On the corrupt path, Nicky invokes Gus's name to sell respectability — the number retirement ceremony as PR — while running the franchise in ways that Gus would have found intolerable.

Gus is either dead or very old during Nicky's tenure. But his presence — the Gus Standard — haunts Nicky's ownership the way gravity haunts a building. The standard exists whether Nicky acknowledges it or not.

### Exit

**Clean path:** Nicky sells reluctantly when the jewelry business declines. His exit is sad, not scandalous. The franchise passes to a new owner cleanly.

**Corrupt path:** Nicky doesn't get indicted — the people behind him do. But the investigation exposes the financial structure, and Nicky's ownership becomes untenable. He's forced to sell under pressure. His exit is humiliating — the most beloved figure in the franchise's public history, exposed as either a willing participant or a useful fool. The debate over which one he was never ends.

## OWNER 4: THE INDICTED FALL GUY (~1978–~1993)

### The Man Left Holding the Bag

### The Figure: Gerald "Gerry Books" DeLuca

**Full Name:** Gerald Francis DeLuca
**Nickname:** Gerry Books (because he's an accountant by training — and because the books are exactly what got him caught)

Gerry DeLuca is not a criminal mastermind. He's a mid-level numbers guy from Cranston who got pulled into the franchise's orbit during the Golden Age because someone needed a financially literate front man when Nicky Gemma started drawing heat. On the clean path, Gerry is a competent but uninspiring local businessman who buys the franchise from Nicky and tries to run it sustainably during a difficult economic period. On the corrupt path, Gerry is the patsy — installed by the connected ownership to manage the books while the real power structure operated from behind the scenes.

### On the Clean Path

Gerry is boring. That's his defining characteristic. After Nicky's charm and largesse, the franchise gets an accountant who runs it like an accounting firm. Budgets are tight. Spending is controlled. The fun evaporates. The players notice. The fans notice. The franchise is solvent but joyless.

Gerry's clean-path challenge is that he's the right owner at the wrong time. The Fall hits — not because of corruption, but because of economics. The mill towns are dying. The fanbase is shrinking. The revenue can't support a competitive roster. Gerry makes the responsible decisions — cutting payroll, trading veterans for prospects, tightening operations — and the franchise gets worse. The fans blame him because he's the face of decline, even though the decline was coming regardless.

He sells in the early 1990s to anyone who'll buy. His exit is quiet. Nobody celebrates his tenure. Nobody mourns it. He was there. It was bad. He left.

### On the Corrupt Path

Gerry inherits a franchise that is wired with explosives. The financial structure Nicky (and the people behind Nicky) built is a house of cards — laundered revenue, phantom investments, shell company ownership layers. Gerry knows enough to keep the books looking normal from the outside. He doesn't know enough to unwind it.

The Investigation Clock that started under Nicky accelerates under Gerry. The RICO-era federal investigations close in. Gerry is not the target — the people behind him are — but he's the one whose name is on the documents. He's the one sitting in the owner's box when the subpoenas arrive.

The indictment is the defining event of his ownership. The franchise's financial crimes are exposed publicly. The connected figures behind Gerry are arrested or flee. Gerry himself is charged with fraud — not for being a mastermind, but for knowing and not speaking. He's the fall guy in every sense.

### Mechanical Impact

**Clean Path:**
- Budget: Shrinking. Gerry cuts spending annually. The player operates with less every year.
- Constraints: Every expenditure must be justified. The farm system is funded minimally. Free agent spending is nearly zero.
- No corruption mechanics. The challenge is survival through austerity.

**Corrupt Path:**
- Budget: Initially generous (Golden Age hangover), then collapsing as the investigation progresses.
- Constraints: The Investigation Clock is in active phase. Transactions are scrutinized. Some moves are locked. The connected figures make increasingly desperate demands.
- **The Fire Sale:** When the indictment drops, Gerry is forced to liquidate assets to fund legal defense and satisfy creditors. Star players are traded for cents on the dollar. The roster is gutted. The farm system is stripped. The franchise hits rock bottom — not gradually, but in a single catastrophic offseason. This is the event that creates the conditions for Danny Couture's era.

### Exit

**Clean path:** Sells quietly. No drama. No legacy.

**Corrupt path:** Indicted. Convicted. The franchise is seized by the league and put into temporary receivership until a new buyer can be found. Gerry goes to a minimum-security federal facility. He serves three years. He comes back to Cranston and opens a tax preparation business. He never attends another Reds game. When reporters call for comment on anniversary pieces about the Golden Age, his wife says he's not available.

## OWNER 5: THE CIANCI-TYPE REINVENTOR (~1993–~2005)

### The Renaissance Man

### The Figure: Vincent "Vinnie Providence" Moretti

**Full Name:** Vincent James Moretti
**Nickname:** Vinnie Providence (self-given, or at least enthusiastically adopted — he puts it on his business cards, which tells you everything)

Vinnie Moretti is not Buddy Cianci. But he's from the same factory. A Providence guy who grew up on Federal Hill, went to PC, got a law degree, made money in real estate development during the 1980s, and arrived at the Reds' doorstep with a vision, a haircut, and an unshakeable belief that he is the most important person in every room.

He buys the franchise out of receivership (corrupt path) or from Gerry DeLuca's estate sale (clean path) with a coalition of investors, a lot of debt, and a promise: "I'm going to make this franchise the centerpiece of Providence's renaissance."

He means it. He also means to make himself famous doing it.

### Who He Is

Vinnie Moretti is the most charismatic owner since Nicky Gemma, but the charisma is different. Nicky's charm was warm — he remembered your name because he cared. Vinnie's charm is performative — he remembers your name because it's useful. He's a politician who never ran for office. He works every room, every handshake, every photo opportunity. He brands himself into the franchise's identity. His face is on the season ticket package. His voice is on the PA system welcoming fans. He gives speeches at every event whether he's invited or not.

He's smart. Genuinely smart. He understands that Providence in the 1990s is ready for a reinvention — WaterFire is happening, the rivers are uncovered, the waterfront is being developed. He sees the franchise as the anchor of that reinvention. A new stadium on the waterfront. A new identity for the city. And himself at the center of all of it.

He is also, fundamentally, a con man. Not a criminal in the Patriarca sense — he doesn't run a crime family. He runs a development company that overleverages, that promises more than it delivers, that uses public money to subsidize private ambition. He's the modern version of the connected charmer: instead of mob money, it's municipal bonds and tax incentives. Instead of laundering through a vending machine business, he's laundering through a public-private partnership. The corruption has evolved but the DNA is the same.

### The Stadium Move

Vinnie Moretti is the architect of the Top-Tier choice. He's the one who forces the question: stay in Pawtucket or move to Providence. His entire ownership model depends on the waterfront park. Without the move, his development deals collapse. His investors bail. His vision evaporates.

**If the player chooses to move:**

Vinnie orchestrates the Providence waterfront deal. Public bonds. Tax incentives. State funding. The machinery of Rhode Island politics fully engaged. The "I know a guy" economy is operational regardless of path — even a clean franchise has to navigate this to get a stadium built in Rhode Island.

### The Lame Duck Gap

The moment the move is announced, the Bandbox becomes a condemned building. Vinnie stops funding maintenance because every dollar now goes to the waterfront project. The Pawtucket fans know they've been abandoned. Attendance craters. The stadium physically deteriorates — leaking roofs, broken seats, failing lights (echoing the Longest Game's flickering towers). The players feel it. The clubhouse is hostile — not from the players toward each other, but from the building itself. Everything is falling apart.

The Lame Duck years (2-3 seasons) are mechanically punitive:
- Attendance drops 40-60%, slashing gate revenue
- Stadium Infrastructure grade plummets, affecting player morale
- Home field advantage disappears — visiting teams aren't intimidated by a condemned building
- Pawtucket loyalists actively hostile — booing not the players but the ownership, visible protests, "STAY" banners in the outfield
- Media coverage turns negative — the Lame Duck becomes a national embarrassment story
- The Gus Standard takes a hit because the community that built the franchise is being abandoned

Elena Martínez is drafted and developed during these years. She emerges from the crucible. The Lame Duck is her origin story — not the shiny waterfront park.

**If the player chooses to stay:**

Vinnie's ownership implodes. His investors pull out because the development deal was the whole point. He's forced to sell — either to a Pawtucket-loyal buyer or to the next owner in the lineage. His exit is bitter and public. He calls Pawtucket "a city that doesn't understand its own potential." Pawtucket responds the way Rhode Island always responds: by taking it personally and proving him wrong.

Mike Duarte's era begins under whoever buys the franchise from Vinnie's wreckage.

### Mechanical Impact

- **Budget:** Initially high — Vinnie brings real money and investor capital. But the money is leveraged against the stadium project. If the move happens, the budget dips severely during the Lame Duck before rebounding when the waterfront park opens. If the move doesn't happen, the budget collapses when Vinnie's investors flee.
- **Constraints:** Vinnie has opinions about EVERYTHING. He wants to approve marketing decisions. He wants input on the uniform redesign. He wants to meet with free agents personally to "sell them on the vision." He's not interfering with the lineup — he's interfering with the brand. His mandates come through as front office directives about image, not strategy.
- **The Hidden Debt:** Vinnie's financial structure is overleveraged. Even if the move happens and the waterfront park opens successfully, the debt service on the bonds is crushing. The franchise's budget in the years after the park opens is better than the Lame Duck but worse than it appears because a chunk of every dollar goes to servicing Vinnie's deals. The player inherits this debt when Vinnie exits.

### Exit

Vinnie's exit is not clean on any path. If the move happened, he stays through the grand opening, takes credit for everything, and then his financial house of cards starts wobbling. The hidden debt surfaces. Investors demand returns that the franchise can't deliver yet. Vinnie is either forced out by his own board or he sells just ahead of the collapse, cashing out while the getting is good and leaving the next owner with the bill.

If the move didn't happen, he's gone within a year of the decision. Bitter. Public. Scorched earth.

Either way, Vinnie Moretti's legacy is the same as Buddy Cianci's: he did real things for the city and he did them for the wrong reasons, and nobody can agree on which part matters more.

## OWNER 6: THE CORPORATE GRIFTER (~2005–~2015)

### The Modern Predator

### The Figure: Bradford "Brad" Whitfield IV

**Full Name:** Bradford Charles Whitfield IV
**Nickname:** None. Brad doesn't do nicknames. Nicknames are for baseball people. Brad is a finance person who happens to own a baseball team.

Brad Whitfield is from Greenwich, Connecticut. He went to Dartmouth. He worked at a private equity firm in Boston. He has never eaten a wiener all the way. He has never had coffee milk. He has been to Rhode Island exactly twice before buying the Reds — once for a sailboat race in Newport, once for a due diligence meeting at a Providence law firm.

He buys the franchise because it's undervalued. The Vinnie Moretti era left the team leveraged but with a new (or renovated) stadium, a media market, and a brand that can be monetized. Brad sees the Reds the way he sees any distressed asset: something to optimize, extract value from, and eventually flip at a premium.

### Who He Is

Brad is not evil. He's not even unpleasant. He's polite, professional, and completely devoid of any emotional connection to the franchise, the city, or baseball itself. He watches games from the luxury suite while checking his phone. He refers to fans as "consumers." He refers to the stadium as "the venue." He refers to the team as "the asset."

He's the first owner in franchise history who doesn't live in Rhode Island. He commutes from Connecticut. His management team is imported — a CEO from a sports marketing firm in Dallas, a CFO from a consulting group in Chicago, a VP of Fan Experience from a theme park company in Orlando. They're all competent. None of them have ever heard of Gus Almeida.

Brad's strategy is textbook private equity: cut costs, increase revenue, service the debt, build equity, sell high. He renegotiates vendor contracts. He raises ticket prices. He introduces dynamic pricing. He sells naming rights to the stadium. He replaces the frozen lemonade family's cart with a corporate beverage sponsor.

That last detail is small and it's enormous. The frozen lemonade family has been at the Reds' ballpark since the 1940s. They served free coffee during the Longest Game. Their spot has been non-negotiable for sixty years. Brad's marketing team runs the numbers and determines that a corporate beverage deal is worth $2 million a year. The frozen lemonade cart is worth nothing on a spreadsheet.

The player receives a front office directive: "The board has approved a new beverage partnership. The existing vendor's contract will not be renewed."

What the player does with this is a second-tier choice. Protect the family or take the money. It's a small decision. It's also a referendum on the franchise's soul, and the Gus Standard knows it.

### The Dead Money Mechanic

Brad Whitfield is the owner who creates the conditions for Jayden Cross. He wants a marketable star. He doesn't care about development or culture — he cares about jersey sales, sponsorship value, and national TV appearances. When the franchise signs Jayden (or whoever the low-Gus-Standard mercenary is), it's Brad's mandate.

When Jayden leaves, Brad's board panics. The sponsors demand a replacement face. The directive comes down: sign a marquee name immediately. The Dead Money contract — overpaid, aging, full no-trade clause mandated by the board for "marketing purposes" — is Brad's legacy mechanic. The franchise is now trapped in a cycle of expensive, declining veterans who exist to sell luxury suites, not win baseball games.

The full NTC is hard-coded. The player cannot trade the albatross. The player cannot buy him out. The player cannot bury him in the minors. He must be on the 25-man roster, playing, visible, because Brad's sponsors are paying for visibility, not wins.

### Mechanical Impact

- **Budget:** High. Corporate money flows. But flexibility is near zero because the budget is allocated by the board, not the front office. The player sees a large number but can only control a fraction of it.
- **Constraints:** Brad's management team issues directives about everything the player would normally control: ticket prices, vendor contracts, marketing calendar, even uniform design. The front office is being managed FROM ABOVE in ways that mirror the corrupt path's loss-of-agency — except instead of mob demands, it's corporate mandates. Different language. Same powerlessness.
- **The Gus Standard Drain:** Brad's ownership is a slow, constant drain on the Gus Standard. Not through dramatic corruption — through bureaucratic indifference. Every decision that replaces tradition with optimization, every vendor replaced by a sponsor, every "heritage night" that reduces the franchise's history to a promotion, is a small cut. The diegetic indicators degrade under Brad not because of villainy but because of irrelevance. The franchise's soul dies of neglect, not violence.

### Exit

Brad sells when the numbers tell him to sell. He's owned the franchise for ten years. The brand equity has increased. The debt has been restructured. The flip is profitable. He sells to either another corporate group (if the Gus Standard is too low for a grassroots alternative) or to the grassroots coalition (if enough community investment materializes).

His exit press conference is professional. He thanks "the great city of Providence" and "the passionate fans" in a tone that is identical to Jayden Cross's departure statement. He means it the same way Jayden meant it — as a pleasantry, not a truth.

He returns to Greenwich. He never thinks about the Rhode Island Reds again.

## OWNER 7: THE GRASSROOTS GROUP (~2015–PRESENT)

### The People's Franchise (Maybe)

### Not One Person — A Coalition

The Grassroots Group is the mirror image of the Mill Coalition that founded the franchise in 1918. Instead of mill owners pooling resources, it's community members, local business owners, and small-scale investors pooling resources. The structure is different. The principle is the same: the franchise belongs to the people who show up.

### Availability

The Grassroots Group's composition and viability depends entirely on the Gus Standard.

**High Gus Standard:** The coalition is strong. Community investment is robust. Local businesses contribute. Former players invest (Elena might buy a small stake; Danny Couture might contribute his savings). The ownership group has the credibility and the funding to operate independently. It's lean, it's underfunded compared to corporate ownership, but it's real.

**Medium Gus Standard:** The coalition exists but it's fragile. Funding is thin. The community wants to believe but has been burned before. Former players are skeptical. The group has enough to operate but no margin for error. One bad season, one financial shortfall, and the whole thing collapses back to corporate ownership.

**Low Gus Standard:** The coalition barely materializes. Community trust is too damaged. Local investors see the franchise as toxic. The group that forms is underfunded, disorganized, and one financial crisis away from selling back to the next Brad Whitfield. On the lowest path, the grassroots group might not form at all — the franchise stays corporate indefinitely.

### The Leader: Maria Sousa-Chen

**Full Name:** Maria Beatriz Sousa-Chen
**Background:** Portuguese-American on her mother's side (Sousa family from East Providence, been in Rhode Island since the 1930s). Chinese-American on her father's side (Chen family from Cranston, arrived in the 1970s). MBA from URI. Worked in community development in Providence for fifteen years. Has never worked in baseball. Has attended Reds games since childhood.

Maria is not a baseball person. She's a civic infrastructure person who understands how communities are built and sustained. She sees the franchise the way Gus saw the Bandbox — as a thing that belongs to the people who use it, maintained properly or it falls apart.

She doesn't have Nicky Gemma's charm or Vinnie Moretti's ambition. She has a spreadsheet, a mission statement, and an unshakeable belief that a baseball franchise can be run like a community development corporation — lean, accountable, and oriented toward the people it serves rather than the people who own it.

She's also learning on the job, which makes her vulnerable. She doesn't know baseball operations. She hires people who do, and she trusts them — which is either her greatest strength (she lets the experts work) or her greatest weakness (she can be misled by people who are smarter about baseball but less principled about the franchise's mission).

### Mechanical Impact

- **Budget:** Small. The smallest in the league. The grassroots model generates enough revenue to operate but not enough to compete on payroll. The player is running a Moneyball operation whether they want to or not.
- **Constraints:** Maria doesn't issue mandates the way previous owners did. She issues VALUES. The front office directive might read: "The board has reaffirmed its commitment to community pricing — ticket prices will not exceed $XX." Or: "The board has approved the restoration of the original vendor partnerships." These constraints are the opposite of Brad's — they limit revenue optimization in favor of community access.
- **The Gus Pipeline:** Under Maria's ownership, the Gus Pipeline is funded and prioritized for the first time since the clean-path Golden Age. Local scouting is emphasized. Development over acquisition. The system that produced Tommy Pickard and Sam Medeiros is given resources and runway.
- **The Vulnerability:** If the team loses badly for multiple consecutive seasons, community patience wears thin. Maria's coalition starts fracturing. Board members who invested expecting a competitive product start pushing for changes. The player has to WIN — not just maintain the culture, but produce results — because the grassroots model can't survive indefinite losing the way corporate ownership can.

### The Gus Standard Under Maria

Maria doesn't know who Gus Almeida is when she takes over. She learns — from the old-timers, from the franchise records, from the diegetic traces left in the organization. Her response to learning about Gus determines whether the grassroots era is a genuine restoration or a nostalgic imitation.

On the high Gus Standard path, Maria inherits a living culture and her job is to protect it while modernizing the infrastructure. The Gus Standard IS the operating philosophy — Maria just gives it a budget and gets out of the way.

On the medium path, Maria inherits fragments and has to piece together what the franchise was supposed to be. She's doing archaeology as much as management. The Gus Standard is a recovery project.

On the low path, Maria inherits almost nothing. The culture has been so thoroughly sanitized by corporate ownership that the Gus Standard is a rumor. She might not even learn the name until someone — the old-timer's granddaughter, a retired player, a beat writer with long memories — tells her. And then she has to decide whether to revive something she never experienced or build something new.

## THE COMPLETE OWNERSHIP LINEAGE

| Era | Owner | Personality | Core Mechanic |

Each owner is the logical product of what came before. Ashworth's collapse creates the need for Sal. Sal's relationships create the conditions for Nicky. Nicky's ambitions (or crimes) create the mess Gerry inherits. Gerry's failure (or austerity) creates the opportunity for Vinnie. Vinnie's overleveraging creates the distressed asset Brad exploits. Brad's extraction creates the community anger that produces Maria. The lineage is a century of cause and effect — and the player's choices shaped every transition.
